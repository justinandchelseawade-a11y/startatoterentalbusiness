What Pages & Features a Tote Rental Website Actually Needs

By Chelsea & Justin Wade — what to build, what to skip, from the site that books our own rentals.

The biggest mistake new tote owners make with a website is building too much. You don't need ten pages, a blog, and a fancy animated logo. You need a visitor to land, instantly understand what you rent and where, trust you, and book. That's it. A focused one-page site beats a sprawling five-page one almost every time.

Here's the short list of what actually earns bookings — and the stuff you can safely ignore at the start.

The 7 things your site needs

1. A hero that answers "what, where, how much" in 3 seconds

The top of your page should make it obvious: you rent moving totes, in [your city/area], starting at [price]. Add one clear button — "Book Now" or "Check Availability." If a stranger can't tell what you do and whether you serve them within a few seconds, they leave.

2. Clear pricing and packages

Nothing kills a booking like "contact us for pricing." Show your packages plainly: how many totes, how many weeks, delivery included or not, the price. People want to picture the cost before they reach out. Even simple tiers (Studio / 1–2 Bed / 3+ Bed) work great.

3. How it works

A short 3- or 4-step rundown removes hesitation: You book → we deliver → you pack & move → we pick up. Renting totes is unfamiliar to a lot of people; showing how simple it is does a lot of the selling.

4. A booking form that lands in your inbox

This is the whole point of the site. One short form — name, dates, address or zip, package — that emails you the moment it's submitted. Keep it short; every extra field costs you bookings. Avoid burying it; it should be reachable from the very top of the page.

5. Your service area

Say exactly where you deliver, by city, county, or zip codes. It saves you fielding "do you come to ___?" messages and it helps you show up in local "tote rental near me" searches.

6. Trust signals

Real photos of your totes (phone photos are fine), a sentence or two about who you are, and any reviews you have. People are letting you deliver to their home — a face and a few kind words go a long way. No reviews yet? A simple "locally owned, here's our story" still builds trust.

7. A short FAQ

Answer the five questions you get asked most: How long is a rental? How many totes do I need? Do you deliver? How clean are they? What if I need them longer? Every question you answer on the page is one less reason to hesitate.

That's genuinely the whole list

Hit those seven and you have a site that converts. Everything past this is polish you can add later once bookings are coming in.

What you can skip (for now)

  • A blog — great for SEO eventually, but not what gets you your first booking. Don't let it delay launch.
  • Multiple pages — one well-organized page usually beats five thin ones. Add an About or service-area page only when you have a reason.
  • Online live availability calendars — nice someday; a booking form you confirm by hand is perfectly fine to start.
  • Fancy animations and a custom logo — clean and clear beats flashy. Spend the energy on photos and pricing instead.

Two things that quietly matter more than design

  • It has to work on a phone. Most of your visitors are on their phone. If the text is tiny or the form is a pain to tap, you lose them. Mobile-first isn't optional.
  • It has to load fast. A heavy, slow page loses people before they ever see your pricing. Big uncompressed photos are the usual culprit.

Frequently asked

Do I need more than one page?

Usually not to start. A single, well-organized page covering the seven essentials converts better than several thin pages a visitor has to dig through.

Should I add a booking calendar with live availability?

It's a nice upgrade later, but not needed at launch. A simple form you confirm by email keeps you in control of your schedule and is plenty for your first customers.

What's the single most important part?

The booking form and clear pricing. If people can see what it costs and reach you in two taps, you've done the job. Everything else supports those two.

I don't have professional photos. Does it matter?

Clear phone photos of your actual totes beat stock images. And if you have none yet, clean tote illustrations keep the site looking polished until you do.
